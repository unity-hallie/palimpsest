A GLSL fragment shader that treats the background as dermis, the text as ink aging inside it, and the whole surface as a slowly breathing body.

Hallie Larsson & Claude · 2026

The idea

Not a metaphor. A model.

Most terminal themes are about color. This one is about tissue. The background color isn't a background — it's a chromophore reading. The text isn't rendered on top of anything; it's embedded in the dermis, the way real tattoo ink is. Everything else follows from that premise.

The shader runs every frame on the GPU. It has no idea what your text says. It only knows pixel colors, cursor position, and time. From those it reconstructs a skin tone, ages the ink, lights the surface, and breathes.

The background hex isn't a color choice. It's a biopsy.

Set your terminal background to a warm parchment and the shader infers fair, cool-undertoned skin. Set it to a deep warm brown and you get high-melanin dermis with different SSS, different bleed behavior, different ink legibility characteristics. The math is continuous — no presets, no skin tone menu.

Chromophore model

Two axes, infinite skin

The shader reads two things from your background color: how much melanin is present (background luminance, inverted) and how warm or cool the undertone is (red minus blue channel). These two values place you on a continuous skin gamut.

Rendering pipeline

What's actually happening each frame

The shader runs six distinct passes in sequence, each building on the last. Tap a layer to see what it contributes.

Skin base Chromophore model → skinBase color. FBM grain at 120Hz. SSS blush.
Melanin and warmth values mix through a three-point gamut (fair → mid → deep) fully continuously. Fine-grain FBM noise adds pore-scale texture. Three gaussian layers of subsurface scattering add the characteristic red-dermis blush that makes skin look lit from within rather than painted.
Body shape Mesh deformation. UV warped to follow a convex back surface.
An 8×6 grid of control points deforms the UV space so text wraps around a curved body surface — specifically the small of the back, with a convex dome at center. Boundary nodes are pinned to zero displacement so edges stay flat. Interior nodes drift on slow independent sinusoids (muscle micro-movement) layered with a shared breathing displacement.
Normal map + AO Surface normals from FBM heightfield. 8-direction horizon AO.
The same FBM used for skin grain is used as a heightfield. Central differences give surface normals that drive the lighting model. Eight-direction horizon sampling approximates ambient occlusion — pores and surface pits are slightly shadowed. The body shape function is baked into the heightfield, so lighting responds to the macro curvature too.
Ink detection Per-pixel ink mask. Color distance from background, not just luminance.
Each pixel is compared to the background color using both luminance difference and full RGB distance. This catches colored terminal text that luma-only detection would miss. A 3×3 AA pass smooths the ink edges. A radial gaussian bleed kernel (scales with age, skips entirely for fresh ink) spreads the ink mark outward into the dermis.
Ink aging Blue shift. Fade toward skin. Both delayed until top third of screen.
Older ink (higher on screen) shifts toward cool blue — the way real tattoo pigment degrades as red-spectrum particles are broken down by the immune system faster than blue ones. The shift is hue-only: luminance is preserved so ink on dark skin doesn't accidentally lighten. The same age gradient also fades ink back toward the skin tone, like very old tattoos becoming a ghost of themselves. Both effects are zero for the bottom two-thirds of the screen and ramp in gently above that.
Lighting 2-minute day/night sun cycle. Incandescent lamp at night. Wrapped Lambert + Blinn-Phong.
A sun arc completes one cycle every two minutes, passing through golden-hour color at low elevation. As it sets, an incandescent lamp fades in from the right with multi-frequency flicker. Lighting uses wrapped diffuse (Lambert * 0.5 + 0.5) for a soft terminator, plus Blinn-Phong specular. The bleed halo behavior flips with melanin: on fair skin it darkens (ink shadows in dermis); on deep skin it lightens (light ink scattering up against dark substrate).

The body breathes

30 seconds. One cycle.

The mesh deformation is driven by a shared breathing waveform — a sum of harmonics with a slight negative bias so the exhale is longer than the inhale. Boundary nodes are pinned; interior nodes expand radially from the small of the back.

One full breath cycle — 30 seconds at rest. Inhale rises faster; exhale stretches longer.

Melanin spots

Freckles aren't objects. They're concentration.

The spots aren't rendered as circles with a special color. They're local increases in the melanin value before the skin gamut is computed — so a freckle on fair skin looks like a fair-skin freckle, and on mid-tone skin it looks like a mid-tone freckle. The chromophore math handles the rest automatically.

90 spots are scattered with a power-law size distribution — most are tiny (freckle-scale), a few are larger (mole-scale). They cluster toward the center of the screen and emerge over the first minute of the session, weighted by spotFair = (1 − melanin)² so they're invisible on deep skin and prominent on fair.

The same chromophore that makes the skin also makes the spots. One model, zero special cases.
Across the gamut

The shader has to work for everyone.

Early versions of the shader were tuned on fair skin and didn't work on dark. The bleed halo darkened ink that was already against a dark background, making it unreadable. SSS added a reddish blush that looked wrong on deep melanin. The blue-shift aging brightened dark-skin ink instead of just cooling its hue.

Each of these required a different fix. The halo direction flips with melanin: on fair skin it darkens (ink sinking), on deep skin it lightens (ink scattering up). The blue shift preserves luminance — only hue changes. SSS strength scales down to near-zero at high melanin. The deep skin tone in the gamut was made dark enough that mid-tone palette colors actually have contrast to work against.

It's not perfect. But it was designed with the full range in mind, not retrofitted after the fact.
