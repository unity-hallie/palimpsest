The idea

Not a metaphor. A model.

Most terminal themes are about color. This one is about tissue. The background color isn't a background — it's a chromophore reading. Text isn't rendered on top of anything; it's embedded in the dermis, the way real tattoo ink is. Everything else follows from that premise.

The shader runs every frame on the GPU. It has no idea what your text says. It only knows pixel colors, cursor position, and time. From those it reconstructs a skin tone, ages the ink, lights the surface, and breathes.

The background hex isn't a color choice. It's a biopsy.

Set your terminal background to a warm parchment and the shader infers fair, cool-undertoned skin. Set it to a deep warm brown and you get high-melanin dermis with different SSS, different bleed behavior, different ink legibility. The math is continuous — no presets, no skin tone menu.

Rendering pipeline

What's happening each frame

Six passes in sequence, each building on the last. Tap a layer to see what it contributes.

Skin base Chromophore model → skin color. FBM grain. SSS blush.
Melanin and warmth mix through a three-point gamut (fair → mid → deep) fully continuously. Fine-grain FBM adds pore-scale texture. Three gaussian SSS layers add the characteristic red-dermis blush that makes skin look lit from within.
Body mesh 8×6 deformation grid. Edges pinned. Interior breathes.
An 8×6 RBF mesh warps UV space so text wraps around the small of a back. Boundary nodes are pinned (edges stay flat); interior nodes drift on slow sinusoids. A shared breath waveform — 30-second organic cycle — drives radial expansion from the back's center.
Normal map + AO FBM heightfield → normals. 8-direction ambient occlusion.
The skin FBM is also a heightfield. Central differences give surface normals for lighting. The body shape function is baked into the heightfield so lighting responds to macro curvature. Eight horizon samples approximate AO — pores and wrinkles sit in slight shadow.
Ink detection Per-pixel ink mask. Color distance, not just luminance.
Each pixel is compared to the background using both luminance difference and full RGB distance — this catches colored terminal text that luma-only detection misses. A 3×3 AA pass softens edges. A radial gaussian bleed kernel (scales with age) spreads ink outward into the dermis.
Ink aging Hue-only blue shift + fade. Delayed until top third of screen.
Older ink (higher on screen) shifts toward cool blue — red pigment fades faster than blue in real tattoos. The shift preserves luminance so ink on dark skin doesn't accidentally lighten. On the same gradient, ink also fades back toward skin tone, like very old tattoos becoming a ghost of themselves. Both effects are zero in the bottom two-thirds.
Lighting 2-minute day/night sun arc. Incandescent lamp at night.
A sun arc completes every two minutes with golden-hour color at low elevation. As it sets an incandescent lamp fades in (right side, warm flicker). Wrapped Lambert diffuse + Blinn-Phong specular. The bleed halo flips with melanin: on fair skin it darkens (ink sinking); on deep skin it lightens (ink scattering up through dark dermis).

Ink age

The body breathes

30 seconds. One cycle.

The mesh is driven by a shared breathing waveform — sum of harmonics with a slight negative bias so the exhale is longer than the inhale. Boundary nodes pinned; interior nodes expand radially from the small of the back.

One full breath cycle — 30 seconds at rest. Inhale rises faster; exhale stretches and lingers.

Melanin spots

Freckles aren't objects. They're concentration.

The spots aren't circles with a special color. They're local increases in the melanin value before the skin gamut is computed — so a freckle on fair skin looks like a fair-skin freckle, and on mid-tone skin it looks like a mid-tone freckle. The chromophore math handles the rest.

90 spots with a power-law size distribution — most are tiny (freckle-scale), a few larger (mole-scale). They cluster toward center and emerge over the first minute, weighted by (1 − melanin)² so they're invisible on deep skin.

The same chromophore that makes the skin also makes the spots. One model, zero special cases.
Across the gamut

The shader has to work for everyone.

Early versions were tuned on fair skin and didn't work on dark. The bleed halo darkened ink that was already against a dark background, making it unreadable. SSS added a reddish blush that looked wrong on deep melanin. The blue-shift aging brightened dark-skin ink instead of just cooling its hue.

Each required a different fix. The halo direction flips with melanin: on fair skin it darkens (ink sinking), on deep skin it lightens (ink scattering up). The blue shift now preserves luminance. SSS strength scales to near-zero at high melanin. The deep skin tones were made dark enough for mid-tone palette colors to have real contrast.

It's not perfect. But it was designed with the full range in mind, not retrofitted after the fact.
