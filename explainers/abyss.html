the organism

A single cell. One photon. One chance.

Dinoflagellates are single-celled marine organisms — half plant, half animal — that have been evolving for 200 million years. Each one carries its own cold-light chemistry, triggered not by will but by touch.

Noctiluca, Pyrocystis, Lingulodinium — all dinoflagellates, all bioluminescent. Each cell membrane contains mechanoreceptors: ion channels that open under physical pressure. A wave. A fin. A hand.

When the channel opens, a voltage spike propagates through the membrane. Protons flood the scintillon — a specialized acidic organelle — dropping the pH and activating luciferase. Luciferin oxidizes. A photon leaves the cell. Blue-green, 474nm.

Then silence. The luciferin is spent. The cell must recharge over hours before it can fire again.

Each cell fires once per disturbance event. The recharge period (hours) prevents re-triggering — which is why disturbed water doesn't just stay lit.

The shader approximation: disturbance uses max() not += — so it can't stack across frames. It rises to meet a spike, then decays. The refractory period isn't fully modeled (a cooldown channel would be the next layer of biology), but the spike-then-decay behavior captures the essential character: touch creates light; the light doesn't outlast the touch by much.
the pipeline

Two passes. Two signals.

The effect runs as a compute + fragment pair. The compute shader runs every frame before the fragment shader sees anything, maintaining a state texture that accumulates history.

The terminal texture is read-only. The shader never modifies what Ghostty renders — it only observes changes and responds with light.

Change detection — each frame, the compute shader compares the current terminal luma at each pixel to the luma stored from the previous frame. A difference means something changed: a character appeared, the cursor moved, text scrolled. This difference is the "disturbance."

To prevent cursor flicker from re-triggering every frame at the same position, the stored luma is lerped toward the current value at 30% per frame — so a single-frame transient only echoes at 30% strength the next frame, 9% the frame after, and so on. Fast typing doesn't oscillate.

Disturbance → emission — disturbance decays fast (~0.15s halflife). It spikes on change and fades. Emission rises toward disturbance but slowly (12% per frame), then decays at its own rate (~0.3s halflife). This creates the characteristic shape: rapid flash, then a longer tail of fading light.

Scroll events are compressed with sqrt(change) — a luma change of 0.64 (full-screen scroll) produces only 0.8× disturbance instead of spiking to maximum. This keeps scroll readable rather than blinding.

Drift — the B channel of the state texture is a self-incrementing timer (increments 0.004/frame, wraps at 2π, giving a ~25 second cycle). Each frame, the velocity field is derived from this timer using smooth sinusoidal functions. Emission is advected — instead of reading the previous emission at the current UV position, the shader reads from slightly upstream: state_read.sample(smp, uv - vel). Light moves with the current.

Bloom — the fragment shader samples the emission field using a golden-angle spiral (24 taps, tight radius) and adds a soft halo. Text is added additively so dark anti-aliased edges contribute nothing — the bloom carries the letter forms rather than masking cutting against it.

the real thing

Milky Sea and wake light.

Bioluminescent bays — Mosquito Bay in Puerto Rico, Luminous Lagoon in Jamaica — have such dense dinoflagellate populations that the water glows blue-green continuously. Disturbed water is brighter; still water dims.

A ship's wake at night is one of the strangest sights in nature. The bow wave spreads out in a V, each compressed water mass triggering cells in sequence. The turbulence at the stern glows longest because it churns the water most. The trail persists for minutes as the emission field drifts and fades.

Your hand in the water becomes visible as its own outline, drawn in living light. Not your hand — the negative-space map of where your hand displaced water.

The Milky Sea is a rarer phenomenon — bacterial bioluminescence rather than dinoflagellate — where thousands of square kilometers of ocean glow faintly and continuously for days. First photographed from space in 2005. The light is so uniform and pervasive that it looks like a flat white surface from orbit.

The shader approximates the dinoflagellate variety: localized, triggered by disturbance, fading over seconds. The Milky Sea would be the ambient floor — always slightly lit, everywhere, no trigger needed. That's the max(emission, 0.012) under static text: the organisms that are always barely alive.
